Introduction




Welcome to your guide to making Japanese food at home!

Let's be real - Japanese cooking can seem tricky when you first start. Maybe you've wondered how to get that perfect sticky sauce on your teriyaki chicken, or how to make those fluffy pancakes that look like clouds. Well, you're in the right place.

This cookbook breaks everything down into simple steps. We've tested these recipes in regular home kitchens, using ingredients you can find at your local grocery store. No fancy equipment needed - just basic pots, pans, and a willingness to try something new.

From quick weeknight meals to weekend cooking projects, you'll find recipes that fit your schedule. Want a fast stir-fry after work? Got it. Planning to wow your friends with homemade ramen? We've got that too. Each recipe comes with helpful tips from home cooks who've made these dishes many times - they know where you might get stuck and how to fix common problems.

The best part? These aren't just copies of restaurant recipes. They're made for home cooking, with clear directions that tell you exactly what to do. We've included notes about which steps you can do ahead of time, what to watch out for, and how to know when something's cooked just right.

Whether you're new to cooking or just new to Japanese food, this book is written for you. Ready to get started? Let's head to the kitchen and make something tasty!





Appetizers & Snacks





1. Takoyaki (Octopus Balls)




These little round snacks are big hits in Japan's food streets. Made famous in Osaka in the 1930s, they're now loved worldwide. With crispy outside and soft inside filled with octopus chunks, they're perfect for sharing. The sauce mix on top makes them extra tasty - once you start, you can't stop!

Preparation Time: 10 minutes Cooking Time: 20 minutes Servings: 1-2 servings Ingredients:

Batter:

1&3/4 cups all-purpose flour



1 pinch dashi stock powder



2 large eggs



1&3/4 cups water





Fillings:

5 oz fresh boiled octopus, chopped



2 tbsp red pickled ginger



1/2 cup tempura flakes



1 bunch spring onion, chopped





Toppings:

1/4 cup takoyaki sauce



1/4 cup katsuobushi (bonito flakes)



1/4 cup Japanese mayonnaise



1 tbsp aosa seaweed powder (or aonori)





Instructions:

Mix your batter. Crack 2 eggs in a bowl, add flour, water, and dashi powder.



Stir until smooth. Set aside.



Get your pan ready. Put your takoyaki pan on medium heat.



Add a tiny bit of oil in each hole.



Start cooking. Drop a piece of octopus in each hole.



Pour batter until it fills up and slightly spills over.



Add the good stuff. Sprinkle spring onions, ginger, and tempura flakes on top.



Time to flip! After 1-2 minutes, use a skewer to turn each ball.



Don't worry if they look messy - they'll shape up.



Keep rolling. Turn them every couple minute until they're golden brown all over (about 3-4 minutes).



Make them pretty. Put balls on a plate. Zigzag the sauces on top. Finish with seaweed powder and bonito flakes. Let them cool a bit - they're super-hot inside!





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Freeze your octopus for 30 minutes before chopping - it makes cutting way easier and gives you neater pieces.



Can't find dashi powder? Use chicken stock powder instead. It's different but still adds good flavor. Just don't tell your Japanese friends!





2. Okonomiyaki




This filling pancake was born in Osaka, Japan. It's a real hit at food stalls and restaurants across the country. The name means "cook what you like" because you can throw in whatever ingredients you want. It's crunchy, savory, and loaded with cabbage and pork - perfect for lunch or dinner when you want something different.

Preparation Time: 15 minutes Cooking Time: 25 minutes Servings: 4 servings Ingredients:

For the Base Batter:

1 cup all-purpose flour



6 oz mountain yam



3/4 cup dashi stock



1/4 tsp kosher salt



1/4 tsp sugar



1/4 tsp baking powder





For the Pancake:

1/2 lb. pork belly, sliced



4 large eggs



1/2 cup tempura bits



1/2 large head green cabbage (about 1.4 lbs.)



1/4 cup pickled red ginger



Cooking oil for frying





For Topping:

Dried seaweed flakes



Pickled red ginger



Green onions



Okonomiyaki sauce



Japanese mayo



Dried bonito flakes





Instructions:

Mix flour, salt, sugar, and baking powder in a bowl.



Peel and grate the mountain yam (watch out - it's slippery and might make your hands itch). Add it to the bowl with dashi. Mix well.



Cut cabbage into tiny bits, wash, and dry it really well.



Chop the ginger. Cut pork into 4-inch pieces.



Add eggs, tempura bits, and most of the ginger to your batter.



Mix in cabbage bit by bit.



Heat oil in a pan over medium heat (400°F).



Pour in enough batter to make a 6-inch circle.



Put 3 pork slices on top. Cover and cook for 5 minutes.



Flip it carefully. Press down a bit. Cover and cook for 5 more minutes.



One last flip, cook 2 minutes uncovered.



Top with sauce, mayo, bonito flakes, seaweed, green onions, and leftover ginger.





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Let the batter sit in the fridge overnight. It makes the pancake super fluffy, just like they do in Japan's food stalls.



Try bacon instead of pork belly - it's easier to find and adds a nice smoky taste. You can also toss in some corn or cheese if you want to make it even more filling.





3. Korokke (Japanese Potato Croquettes)




These potato balls come from Japan, where they're super popular as street food and home cooking. They're basically mashed potatoes mixed with onions, coated in crispy bread crumbs, and fried until golden. If you like french fries or potato wedges, you'll love these little crispy treats.

Preparation Time: 30 minutes Cooking Time: 15 minutes Servings: 8 large pieces Ingredients:

For Frying:

2 cups vegetable oil





Potato Mix:

6 medium russet potatoes (about 2 pounds), peeled and cut in quarters



1 tbsp vegetable oil



2-3 tsp curry powder (if you want it)



1 small yellow onion (about 1 cup when diced)



1 tsp salt



1/4 tsp black pepper



1 tsp garlic powder





Coating:

2 large eggs, beaten



1&1/2 cups panko breadcrumbs



1/4 cup cornstarch





Dipping Sauce:

5 tsp Worcestershire sauce



2&1/4 tsp white sugar



2 tbsp ketchup



1 tbsp stir fry sauce (or oyster sauce)





Instructions:

Put the potato quarters in a pot with water and boil them for 15 minutes until you can easily stick a fork through them. Drain them in a strainer.



While the potatoes cook, grab a pan and cook your diced onions with 1 tbsp oil until they're soft and clear-looking.



Put the potatoes in a bowl, add the cooked onions, salt, garlic powder, and pepper. If you like curry flavor, add that too.



Mash everything together until well mixed. Split the mix into 8 equal parts and roll each into oval shapes. Put them in the fridge for 15 minutes - this helps them stay together better when you coat them.



Get 3 bowls ready: put cornstarch in one, beaten eggs in another, and panko in the last one.



Take each potato ball and roll it first in cornstarch, then dip in egg, and finally cover it with panko crumbs.



Pour your oil in a big pan and heat it up on medium. To check if it's hot enough, stick a wooden chopstick or spoon handle in - if bubbles form around it, you're good to go.



Fry the balls for about 1 1/2 minutes on each side until they're golden brown.



For the Sauce: Mix all sauce ingredients in a bowl until smooth.





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Add a handful of frozen corn kernels to the potato mixture. It adds little bursts of sweetness that Japanese people love.



Pro move: After shaping the balls but before coating them, stick a small cube of cheese in the middle. When fried, you'll get an awesome melty center.





4. Grilled Eggplant




This is a quick Japanese dish that people love to make at home. The eggplant gets a nice smoky taste from being cooked right on the flame. It's super easy to make and needs just a few ingredients. The ginger and soy sauce make it taste fresh and sharp. You'll want to make it again and again.

Preparation Time: 2 minutes Cooking Time: 10 minutes Servings: 2 servings Ingredients:

2 medium eggplants



2 tbsp soy sauce



1/4 cup katsuobushi (dried bonito flakes)



1 tsp fresh ginger root, grated





Instructions:

Take your ginger and grate it finely. Set it aside.



Cut off the green tops (calyx) from your eggplants.



Set your gas stove to medium-high heat. Use tongs to set the eggplant straight on the flame.



Cook for about 4-5 minutes total. Keep turning the eggplant every 30 seconds or so to make sure all sides get charred evenly.



Put the cooked eggplant on a plate. Top with the grated ginger, pour soy sauce over it, and finish with a generous sprinkle of katsuobushi flakes.





Special Notes:

If you want the skin extra crispy, pat the eggplant dry with paper towels before grilling. The drier the skin, the better it will char.



No gas stoves? You can use a grill pan or broiler. Just prick the eggplant's skin with a fork first so it doesn't explode while cooking. Cook it for about 3 minutes on each side until soft.





5. Tamagoyaki (pan fried rolled egg or rolled omelette)




This breakfast egg roll comes from Japan, where people make it every morning. It's basically a rolled-up omelet, but way prettier and tastier than regular ones. The touch of soy sauce and sweet wine makes it special. Once you try it, you'll want to make it again and again.

Preparation Time: 3 minutes Cooking Time: 7 minutes Serving size: 2 servings Ingredients:

4 large eggs



1 tbsp mirin (or 1/4 tsp sugar)



1/4 tsp soy sauce



1 tsp vegetable oil



1/4 tsp salt





Instructions:

Break eggs into a bowl. Add salt, soy sauce, and mirin (or sugar if using). Beat everything together until well mixed.



Get your pan hot over medium-high heat. Add oil and spread it around. A rectangle pan works best, but any pan will do the job.



Here comes the fun part:





Pour just enough egg mix to make a thin layer in the pan



Wait until the bottom sets but the top is still a bit wet



Start at one end and roll it up like a sleeping bag



Move your egg roll to one end



Pour more egg mix into the empty space



Let it set a bit, then roll your first piece over the new layer



Keep doing this until you've used all your egg mix





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Put your eggs in warm water for 5 minutes before cracking them. This helps them cook more evenly and makes rolling easier.



Want to change it up? Add a sprinkle of furikake (Japanese seasoning) or finely chopped green onions between layers. It adds fun spots of color when you cut into it.





6. Senbei (Japanese Rice Crackers)




These crackers came from Japan where people love them as snacks. They're super crunchy and slightly salty - perfect with tea or beer. Made from rice flour and simple seasonings, these crackers are now popular worldwide. Once you try them, you'll want to make them again and again.

Preparation Time: 20 minutes Cooking Time: 20 minutes Servings: 4 servings Ingredients:

For the Crackers:

1 cup rice flour or mochiko



1/4 cup cooked white rice



2 tbsp vegetable oil



1/4 cup water



1/4 tsp sea salt





For the Glaze:

2 tsp mirin



1 tbsp soy sauce





Optional Toppings:

3 tsp black sesame seeds



2 tsp shichimi togarashi (red chili pepper mix)



5 tsp furikake rice seasoning



Nori seaweed sheets





Instructions:

Get your oven hot at 375°F. Get two baking sheets ready with parchment paper or oil them up. Mix soy sauce and mirin in a small bowl for later.



Mix your cracker stuff: Put rice flour, cooked rice, salt, and oil in your food processor. Run it until everything's ground up fine. Add water bit by bit until you get a crumbly mix that's just a little wet.



Dump everything in a bowl. Add whatever toppings you picked. Squish it all together with your hands until it makes dough. It'll feel kind of loose - that's okay. Just keep pressing it. Make same-size balls with the dough.



Make your crackers: Put each dough ball between plastic wrap. Squash it flat with a rolling pin until it's about 2.5 inches wide. If it's too sticky, just shape them by hand. Peel off the plastic and put them on your baking sheet, leaving a little space between each one.



Cook one sheet at a time for 8-10 minutes. Flip them over and cook for another 8-10 minutes until they start getting brown.



Take them out, let them cool for a minute. Brush the tops with your soy sauce mix. Put them back in for 2-3 minutes until they're light brown. Watch them like a hawk - they burn fast!



Let them cool on a rack - they'll get crunchier as they sit. Keep them in a container for a week tops. Want extra flavor? Wrap them in nori.





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Try grinding some dried bonito flakes (katsuobushi) into powder and mix it into your dough. It adds an amazing savory taste that makes these crackers even better.



For extra crunch, put your finished crackers back in the turned-off oven while it's cooling down. They'll dry out more and get super crispy.





Salads





7. Cucumber Salad (Vinegar Dressing)




This is a light Japanese side dish that's been around forever. It's super popular in Japanese homes and restaurants because it's quick to make and tastes fresh. The mix of crunchy cucumber and soft seaweed with the tangy dressing makes it a perfect match for any meal. If you like pickles, you'll love this.

Preparation Time: 10 minutes Cooking Time: 15 minutes Servings: 2 servings Ingredients:

1 cup thinly sliced cucumber



2 tbsp dried cut wakame seaweed



1/2 tsp salt





For the vinegar dressing:

2 tbsp dashi stock



1 tbsp mirin



2 tbsp rice wine vinegar



1 tbsp soy sauce





Instructions:

Put the dried wakame in a bowl with water. Let it sit for 10-15 minutes until it gets soft and puffy. Drain all the water out. If the seaweed pieces look too big, cut them into 1-inch pieces.



Put the cucumber slices in a bowl and sprinkle salt on them. Use your hands to squeeze and mix them around - this helps make them tender. Keep squeezing until you get most of the water out. Add these cucumber pieces to your seaweed bowl.



Mix up your dressing in another bowl. Just put in the dashi stock, soy sauce, mirin, and rice vinegar. Stir it all together.



When you're ready to eat, pour the dressing over your cucumber and seaweed. Mix everything well and serve right away.





Special Notes:

Want it extra crunchy? Put your cucumber slices in an ice bath for 5 minutes before salting them. This makes them super crisp.



Try adding a pinch of toasted sesame seeds or a drop of sesame oil at the end - it's not traditional but adds a nice nutty flavor that lots of people love.





8. Goma-ae (Spinach with Sesame Dressing)




This Japanese side dish puts spinach in the spotlight with a nutty sesame dressing. It's a popular dish in Japan, served cold and perfect for hot summer days. The sesame sauce is creamy and rich, making plain spinach taste amazing. Even kids who don't like vegetables might change their minds after trying this!

Preparation Time: 10 minutes Cooking Time: 5 minutes Servings: 4 servings Ingredients:

1 pound baby spinach



2 tbsp reduced-sodium soy sauce



1 tsp mirin



1 tsp white sugar



2 tbsp sesame seeds, plus extra for topping



2 tbsp tahini



1 tbsp rice vinegar





Instructions:

Fill a big pot with water and turn up the heat until it boils. Split your spinach into two piles. Drop the first pile in the water for 30 seconds - just until it turns bright green and soft. Fish it out and do the same with the second pile. Put all the cooked spinach in a strainer and run cold water over it until it's completely cool.



Get your sesame seeds ready - grind them up in a coffee grinder or small food processor until they're crushed but not totally powdered. Mix these ground seeds in a bowl with the tahini, rice vinegar, soy sauce, and mirin. Keep stirring until it looks thick like mayo. If you want to make it look nice when serving, save a spoonful or two of this dressing.



Take your spinach and give it a good squeeze to get rid of extra water. You can use a clean kitchen towel or paper towels to pat it dry. Toss the spinach in the bowl with your sesame dressing until every leaf is coated. Put it on a nice plate or split it between four small bowls. If you save some dressing, drizzle it on top and sprinkle it with whole sesame seeds.





Special Notes:

Try blanching the spinach in dashi stock instead of plain water - it adds an extra layer of umami flavor that works great with the sesame dressing.



For a spicy kick, add a few drops of chili oil or a pinch of Japanese seven-spice powder (shichimi togarashi) on top before serving.





9. Kani Salad




This is a popular Japanese-American dish that you'll find in many sushi restaurants. It's a mix of sweet imitation crab, fresh cucumber, and mango, all dressed up in a creamy sauce. The best part? It takes only 15 minutes to make and tastes super fresh. Perfect for hot summer days!

Preparation Time: 15 minutes Cooking Time: 0 minutes Serving Size: 2 servings Ingredients:

For the Slaw:

2 Persian or Lebanese cucumbers



1 cup mango, fresh or frozen



8 oz imitation crab meat sticks





For the Dressing:

3-4 tbsp Kewpie Japanese mayonnaise



1 tsp rice vinegar



2&1/2 tsp soy sauce



1 tsp sriracha sauce (add more or less for heat)





For Topping:

1/2 avocado



2 tsp panko breadcrumbs



2 tsp sesame seeds, toasted





Instructions:

Cut your cucumbers in half the long way. Then slice them into super thin strips, like matchsticks.



Take each crab stick and pull it apart into thin strings with your fingers (like string cheese). This works better than using a knife.



Cut up your mango into thin strips and toss it in with your cucumber and crab.



Mix up your dressing in a bowl - start with 3 tbsp mayo, add soy sauce, vinegar, and sriracha. Pour it over your slaw and mix everything well. If it looks dry, add that extra tbsp of mayo.



Split the slaw between two bowls. Top each with some sliced avocado, then sprinkle with sesame seeds and panko for some crunch.





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Pop your imitation crab in the freezer for 10 minutes before shredding - it makes it much easier to pull apart into nice, neat strings.



Try adding some julienned green apple for extra crunch and a sweet-tart kick. It works great with the mango and adds another layer of freshness.





10. Namul Namasu (Daikon and Carrot Salad)




This fresh and tangy side dish comes straight from Japanese cooking. It's a mix of daikon radish and carrots that are cut into thin sticks and dressed with a sweet vinegar sauce. People love it because it's crunchy, easy to make, and goes well with any Asian meal.

Preparation Time: 15 minutes Cooking Time: 0 minutes Servings: 4 servings Ingredients:

2 oz carrots, peeled and cut into 2-inch matchsticks



9 oz daikon radish, peeled and cut into 2-inch matchsticks



2 tsp salt





Dressing:

3 tbsp rice vinegar



3 tbsp sugar



2 tbsp water



1/4 tsp salt





Instructions:

Put the daikon matchsticks and carrot matchsticks in a bowl. Sprinkle with 2 tsp of salt and mix well. Let them sit for 10 minutes.



While waiting, make the dressing. In a small bowl, mix sugar, rice vinegar, water, and 1/4 tsp salt until the sugar dissolves completely.



After 10 minutes, gently squeeze out excess water from the vegetables using your hands or a clean kitchen towel.



Pour the dressing over the squeezed vegetables and mix everything well.



You can eat it right away, but it tastes even better after sitting in the fridge for about 30 minutes.





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Add a small piece of kombu (dried kelp) to the finished dish and let it sit in the fridge. It adds an amazing umami flavor that makes the dish taste more authentic.



Try adding thin strips of red bell pepper for extra color and crunch. The sweetness of the bell pepper works really well with the tangy dressing.

11. Japanese Potato Salad




This is Japan's take on potato salad that's super popular at home parties and picnics. It's not your regular mayo-heavy potato salad - this one's lighter, with a mix of crunchy veggies and a secret ingredient called mentaiko (fish roe) that adds a nice salty kick. People love it because it's refreshing and goes well with any meal.

Preparation Time: 15 minutes Cooking Time: 30 minutes Servings: 4-6 servings Ingredients:

2 large russet potatoes (1 pound), peeled and cut into chunks



2 scallions, sliced thin



5 tbsp Kewpie mayonnaise



2 large eggs



1 tbsp rice vinegar



2 tsp sugar



1 small cucumber (about 4 oz), sliced thin



1 small carrot (2-3 oz), sliced thin



1/2 cup corn kernels (frozen and thawed, or fresh boiled)



1/4 cup chopped radish (about 2 radishes)



1 whole sac mentaiko (2 oz)



1 tsp lemon zest



1 tsp lemon juice



Salt as needed



Black pepper to taste





Instructions:

Get your potatoes going first. Put them in a pot with cold water (make sure they're covered by an inch) and add some salt. Once it's boiling, drop in your eggs. Turn down the heat to low, cover the pot, and let it cook for about 10-12 minutes until you can easily stick a knife through the potatoes.



When done, drain everything. Put the potatoes on a baking sheet, and quickly sprinkle them with the vinegar and sugar while they're still hot. Let them cool down completely (15 minutes at least). Put the eggs in ice water and peel them once they're cool.



While waiting, put your sliced cucumber in a big bowl with some salt. Just let it sit there until your potatoes are ready.



Once potatoes are cool, add them to your cucumber bowl. Use a fork to mash them up a bit - you want some chunks left, not totally smooth.



Now for the fun part - mix in everything else: your carrot, corn, radish, scallions, mayo, mentaiko, lemon zest and juice. Break up the eggs with your hands (or chop them if you prefer) and add those too. Mix it all up and taste it. Add more salt, pepper, sugar, vinegar, lemon juice, or mayo if you think it needs it.





Special Notes:

Want to make it extra creamy? After mashing the potatoes, let them sit in the fridge for 30 minutes. The starch will develop and make everything super smooth when you mix in the mayo.



If you can't find mentaiko, try mixing in a tbsp of smoked salmon or even some canned tuna - it gives that same nice seafood flavor that makes this salad special.

12. Miso Soup (with Greens and Tofu)




This Japanese soup has been around for centuries and is loved worldwide. It's a warm, comforting bowl that takes just minutes to make. The mix of miso paste with fresh greens and tofu makes it healthy and filling. You'll want to make this whenever you need something quick and cozy.

Preparation Time: 5 minutes Cooking Time: 10 minutes Servings: 2 servings Ingredients:

4 cups vegetable broth



1/2 cup chopped green chard



1/2 cup chopped green onion



1/4 cup firm tofu, cut into small cubes



1 sheet nori (about 1/4 cup when cut)



3-4 tbsp white or yellow miso paste





Instructions:

Pour the vegetable broth into a pot. Turn the heat to medium-low and wait until you see tiny bubbles forming.



Take a small bowl and put in your miso paste (start with 3 tbsp). Add a splash of hot water and stir it well with a spoon until it's smooth like paint. This stops the miso from making lumps in your soup. Put this bowl aside for now.



Drop your chopped chard, green onions, and tofu cubes into the warm broth. Let them cook and get soft for about 5 minutes. Then tear up your nori sheet and toss it in. Give everything a quick stir.



Turn off the heat. Now pour in your miso mixture and stir it around. Take a small taste - if you want it more salty, add another tbsp of miso or a tiny pinch of salt. Serve it right away while it's nice and hot.





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Keep your miso paste in the freezer - it stays fresh longer and you can easily scrape off just what you need with a spoon.



Try adding a handful of fresh mushrooms (any kind works) when you put in the greens. They make the soup even more tasty and filling.





13. Tonjiru (Pork and Vegetable Miso Soup)




This is a filling Japanese stew that combines tender pork with root vegetables in a rich miso broth. It started as a farmers' meal in northern Japan and became super popular across the country. When it's cold outside, this hot bowl of goodness will warm you right up and keep you full for hours.

Preparation Time: 15 minutes Cooking Time: 45 minutes Servings: 6 servings Ingredients:

1/2-pound pork belly slices



6 cups dashi stock



1 tbsp vegetable oil



2 green onions



2 medium Yukon gold potatoes (10 oz)



1 large onion (8 oz)



1 cup daikon radish (5 oz)



1 large carrot (5 oz)



5 oz burdock root



5 oz konjac/konnyaku



1/2 cup miso paste





For Serving (if you want):

Japanese citrus chili paste



Japanese seven spice powder





Instructions:

Get Your Veggies Ready

Cut potatoes into matchsticks and soak in water for 15 minutes



Slice onion thinly



Cut daikon and carrots into matchsticks



Scrape burdock root with a knife back (don't peel!), cut into thin slices, soak 15 minutes



Cut konjac into strips, boil 3 minutes, drain



Slice semi-frozen pork belly into bite-sized pieces





Cook Everything Together

Heat oil in your pressure cooker



Cook onions and pork until pork starts to brown



Throw in all vegetables and konjac



Pour in dashi stock



Pressure cook on high for 1 minute



Let it sit for 30 minutes after cooking



Open lid and check if vegetables are soft





Finish It Up

Mix in miso paste using a ladle



Taste and add more miso if needed



Top with chopped green onions



Add spice powder or chili paste if you like it hot





Special Notes:

Secret Tip 1: Freeze your pork for just 30 minutes before cutting - it makes slicing way easier and gives you neater pieces.



Secret Tip 2: Want to make it even better? Drop in a handful of sliced mushrooms (shiitake or button) with the vegetables. They'll soak up all the good flavors and make the soup even more filling.





14. Zoni (New Year’s Mochi Soup)




This warm soup comes from Japan, where people eat it on New Year's Day to bring good luck. The chewy rice cakes float in a tasty broth made from seaweed and fish flakes. It's a light but filling meal that makes you feel cozy and happy. Japanese families have been making this soup for hundreds of years.

Preparation Time: 30 minutes Cooking Time: 30 minutes Servings: 2 servings Ingredients:

For the Soup:

2 cups dried bonito flakes (katsuobushi)



3 tbsp soy sauce



2 chicken thighs, cut into bite-sized pieces



4 cups water



2 pieces dried sea kelp (kombu)





For the Rice Cakes:

1/2 cup water



1 cup sweet rice flour





Optional Toppings:

Japanese wild parsley (mitsuba)



Japanese fish cake (kamaboko)





Instructions:

Make the Soup Base:

Put 4 cups cold water in a pot and add the sea kelp. Let it sit for at least 30 minutes, but better if overnight.



Heat the pot with kelp on low heat for 15 minutes.



Turn off the heat. Take out the kelp and put in the bonito flakes. Wait 10 minutes.



Strain the liquid - this is your soup base.



Pour the soup base back in the pot. Add soy sauce and chicken pieces.



Cook until chicken is done, about 5-10 minutes.





Make the Rice Cakes:

Mix sweet rice flour and water in a bowl until it feels like play-dough.



Roll small pieces into 1-inch balls and flatten to 1/2 inch thick.



Drop the rice cakes into the soup.



Cook until they float up, then 2 more minutes.



Add your favorite toppings and serve hot.





Special Notes:

2 tbsp sugar



1/4 tsp cream of tartar





For Topping:

Maple syrup



Fresh fruits



Powdered sugar





Instructions:

Split the Eggs

Put the whites in one bowl and yolks in another



Keep the whites super clean - no yolk allowed!





Make the Fluffy Base

Put cream of tartar in with the whites



Beat until foamy with your mixer



Add sugar bit by bit while mixing



Keep beating until the whites stand up tall





Mix the Wet Stuff

Stir milk and vanilla into the yolks



Add flour and baking powder



Mix until smooth





Combine Everything

Put half the white fluff into the yolk mix



Fold gently



Add the rest and fold until just mixed





Cook with Care

Heat pan on low-medium



Lightly oil the pan



Drop 1/4 cup batter twice for each pancake



Cover and cook 1 minute





The Flip

Check if bottom is set



Carefully roll them over



Cook 3-4 more minutes covered





Serve Right Away

Add sugar, fruits, and syrup



Eat while they're still tall and proud





Special Notes:

Secret tip: Add a tiny pinch of salt to make them taste even better



Pro move: Put a few drops of lemon juice in if you can't find cream of tartar - it works the same way!
